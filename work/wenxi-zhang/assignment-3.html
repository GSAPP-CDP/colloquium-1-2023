You've visited New York. And if you haven't visited New York you might have an idea about what a visit to New York would be like, an imagined track of sites and experiences. Does anyone travel through a unique New York? We asked ChatGPT to generate an itinerary, and then found three ways through three mornings/evenings/nights in the city.

We asked ChatGPT to generate a plan for three days in New York, and asked DALL-E to visualize various points along the way. We started with photos contributed to google maps and similar location intelligence or place-based digital platforms (THE BIG AGGREGATE) and asked ChatGPT to help us contextualize more experiential, sensorial, or affective threads within the very same photos and reviews (UNIQUE NEW YORK). Closing the loop, we then simply asked DALL-E to create its own scrapbook of the itinerary ChatGPT devised for it (CHATGPT AND THE CITY) – revealing an uncanniness between user contributed images and those generated by AI.

How do you author an experience? And how do you author a place? A visit to NYC, mediated by a model trained on massive amounts of media generated by millions of visits to NYC (66 million visited in 2019 alone) reveals patterns and limits in how places on a tourist’s itinerary are seen, experienced, and shared. Photos of NYC landmarks tend to look the same, and many visitors and residents likely have very similar experiences of sites like Times Square, but there are multitudes here, in the very same place, that may elude AI modeling – and they’re important.

Each take on the ChatGPT-produced itinerary mirrors some of the limited avenues for authorship that tourists following an itinerary might face. What are all of the branching decision points within the very first prompt “Arrive in NYC”? What are the linguistic, class, racial, experiential intersections with what it means to “arrive” in a place that you’re visiting, especially one as complex as the city.

There is an algorithmic logic to traveling through NYC, and even more so by AI prompt. Sites have been made legible for selling and showing off the city for visitors for century, and they’re a vital part of what makes New York New York.

Wenxi's Reading Response

I see generative AI as a powerful tool. Now the two main types of generative AI could output text or images. In my opinion, most of the time, conversations with generative AI this is just like situations in visual studios, which has no different than a designer searching for inspiration from online readings or glancing at design works from bechance. AI could provide more help with relevant research or image reference gathering and debugging. Sharing something from social media to friends doesn't mean having any authorship, even sharing with comments wouldn’t change the fact. The authorship inside this process is creating a comment or insight on a specific topic, not the video or image itself. As a result, directly posting outcomes from AI output doesn’t count as having ownership or authorship. AI currently could be hard to be seen as an independent creator. Generative Ai is more like a platform. For example, platforms like google, Behance or GitHub won't take the authorship of any context they provide. Thus, directly delivering outputs from AI doesn’t means authorship, it's more like a sharing process.

The decision about when and how to use the AI is essential. These decisions may affect the definition of whether to have authorship or not. Retouch and redesigning the AI outcome means a certain amount of individual thinking process and creation. The debating middle point of retouching might happen when an individual is using AI for most of the working outcome and just has a subtle amount of change. After the retouch, the output will contain a certain amount of authorship, just like defining authorship in a collage. But the percentage of authorship really depends on different scenarios and specific examples.

Having overall authorship means using generative AI just as a reference tool, no other clear visual or text representation of other people's copyright could be detected. The percentage of authorship in this topic really depends on what output we are looking at. Meanwhile, attribution and transparency need to take into consideration. But it's understandable when AI generates information and images, it's hard to really give references, not like collage art or noting references in a research paper.
