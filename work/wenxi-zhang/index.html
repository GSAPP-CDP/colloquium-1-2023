1 – Systems

System of a specific trip

A system mapping of international travel to NYC.

What is the system?

The prologue of parents coming to visit F-1 students to explore NYC the first time as a tourist.

Each year during graduation season, a significant amount of B1/B2 visa holders will come to visit. A specific similar travel pattern could be seen and catalyzed in a group, that parents travel overseas to visit F-1 students for their graduation ceremony. New York is a world-renowned tourist city, and tourist places like Time Square, Statue of Liberty are commonly chosen. As a result, even for parents visiting from all other countries, a system with purpose could be seen in this short-term travel process.

Whom does it impact?

Who: This system shows a clear pattern in location, time, and overall experience. During summer time graduation season, a lot of F-1 students' parents will come to travel to New York and do a short-term visit, which is usually within a month. Elements: in this system involves international travel/locations (stay / tour) / transportation/food/shopping/photos. Purpose: In this system, the participants (parents) have a clear purpose which is to visit students and visit Manhattan NYC. One feedback loop could be seen as how this specific tourist spends their one single day in NYC. (They might speed up the tourist process or delay)

Stocks would be the overall tourist and visiting experience, while inflow would be money and time spent, and the outflow would be photos and experiences. Frequency and delay is also included in this system. Frequency can be defined as a daily base of the tour, while delay can be defined as not visiting any place in one day.

"Clear travel patterns could be seen when parents from various different countries comes to visit…"

What are its leverage points?

Building on top of these, Leverage points are dependence that could influence the flow, in this situation, will be trip planning, transportation methods, and how long to stay at one place.

The hierarchy in this system wouldn't be that obivious, but usually, the F-1 student will plan the overall trip and do the decision-making. Travel Boundaries will be inside NYC. Resilience in this specific system would be the amount of rest that this group needs and overall travel budgets.

How do you author an experience? And how do you author a place? A visit to NYC, mediated by a model trained on massive amounts of media generated by millions of visits to NYC (66 million visited in 2019 alone) reveals patterns and limits in how places on a tourist’s itinerary are seen, experienced, and shared. Photos of NYC landmarks tend to look the same, and many visitors and residents likely have very similar experiences of sites like Times Square, but there are multitudes here, in the very same place, that may elude AI modeling – and they’re important.

Each take on the ChatGPT-produced itinerary mirrors some of the limited avenues for authorship that tourists following an itinerary might face. What are all of the branching decision points within the very first prompt “Arrive in NYC”? What are the linguistic, class, racial, experiential intersections with what it means to “arrive” in a place that you’re visiting, especially one as complex as the city.

There is an algorithmic logic to traveling through NYC, and even more so by AI prompt. Sites have been made legible for selling and showing off the city for visitors for century, and they’re a vital part of what makes New York New York.

3 – Politics

Goals, Methods and Tools

A spatial analysis with multiple forms of travel data.

Goals:

One significant pain point for tourists visiting New York City is the discrepancy between the advertised and actual locations of tourist attractions. Advertisements often depict attractions as being conveniently located and easily accessible, creating an expectation that they are within close proximity to each other. However, the reality can be quite different. NYC is a sprawling metropolis, and attractions can be spread out across different boroughs, requiring significant travel time and navigation through complex public transportation systems. This discrepancy can lead to frustration, wasted time, and missed opportunities to fully experience what the city has to offer. It can be particularly challenging for first-time visitors or those with limited time, who may find themselves spending more time in transit than actually enjoying the attractions. This issue underscores the need for more accurate and realistic representation of locations in tourism advertising.




Methods:

1.Affordances of Location Data: The location data provides several affordances or action possibilities. Users can view detailed information about the location, obtain navigation instructions to reach the location, and view reviews and photos from other users who have visited the same location. These affordances allow users to interact with the location data in meaningful ways, enhancing their understanding and experience of the location. Affordances of Image Data: Similarly, the image data also offers a range of affordances. Users can view detailed information about the image, zoom in or out to see the image more clearly, swipe to view more images, share the image with others, or save the image for future reference. These affordances enable users to engage with the image data, enriching their visual experience and interaction with the content. 2.Reliability: Location data according to tripadvisor and googlemap reviews. Image data comes from location based popular posts. 3.Accessibility: Tourist reviews data are public, but getting personal narrative feedback is less accessible. 4.Detail: Famous tourist places' reviews are more detailed, compared to small shops. 5.Scalability: Depend on organizing data based on location or based on images.

Quantitative data: The travel transportation times, destinations, and durations of stay of travelers, can be collected through online travel platforms and social media. The affordances of these data lie in their accuracy and scale, which can be used to identify patterns and trends in travel behavior.

Qualitative data: Feedback and comments from travelers, can be collected through interviews and online reviews. The affordances of these data lie in their ability to provide in-depth insights into the behaviors and motivations of travelers. By combining these two types of data, I can gain a more comprehensive understanding of travel patterns in NYC, thereby optimizing my system.

4 – Governance

a design fiction about a system's future governance.

A profile of governance.

Cycle City

The city of the future is experiencing transformative changes, driven by new mobility options and shared public transport, with a notable focus on cycling and bike sharing in urban areas. These changes prioritize sustainable and eco-friendly transportation, aiming to reduce congestion and enhance overall livability. Dedicated bike lanes and cycling infrastructure have become integral features of the urban landscape, encouraging more people to adopt cycling as a mode of transport. As a result, streets and urban spaces are being redesigned to prioritize pedestrians and cyclists, reducing car dominance and promoting healthier lifestyles while lowering carbon emissions and improving air quality. Data collection from bikes and users plays a crucial role in optimizing traffic flow and enhancing the transportation system. Real-time data analysis helps identify high-demand areas, enabling city planners to make informed decisions about infrastructure improvements and resource allocation. While the idea of autonomous bicycles offers exciting possibilities for safety and accessibility, there are challenges in integrating them seamlessly with human-driven traffic and ensuring their reliability and security. Overall, achieving a sustainable and efficient cityscape involves balancing shared and individual transportation options while prioritizing safety and addressing data privacy concerns. The introduction of more bike lanes and reduced car usage in urban areas fosters vibrant street-level interactions and enables various activities and services. Pedestrian-friendly zones emerge, encouraging outdoor events, markets, and community gatherings. The quieter, less polluted environment attracts businesses to extend services outdoors, boosting the local economy. In regions with extreme climates, covered or climate-controlled bike lanes and pedestrian areas ensure year-round usability. Integration of bike sharing with public transport offers a sustainable last-mile solution. Overall, these changes enhance the city's livability, encourage multi-modal transportation, and create resilient urban spaces.



Design Fiction:

A Day in the Life of a Cyclist in 2035 In the year 2035, the cityscape has been transformed to prioritize cycling and outdoor activities. As the sun rises, Ava, a fitness enthusiast and environmental advocate, begins her day. Ava's smart home system gently wakes her up with a simulation of a sunrise, accompanied by a soft melody. After a quick breakfast, she prepares for her daily commute to work. She steps into her weather-adaptive cycling suit, a marvel of textile technology that adjusts its temperature and texture based on the weather conditions, ensuring she is always comfortable during her ride. She heads to the garage where her smart bike awaits. The bike, equipped with an AI-powered navigation system, has already planned the most efficient route to her workplace. The navigation system is designed specifically for cyclists, taking into account bike lanes, traffic conditions, and even the scenic value of the route. As Ava starts her journey, the city's infrastructure caters to her needs. The smart roads detect her presence and illuminate the bike lanes for better visibility. The city's weather control system, a network of weather modification drones, ensures a clear sky for cyclists, making rainy or snowy commutes a thing of the past. During her ride, Ava's bike also doubles as a fitness device. It tracks her vitals, the calories burned, and even her posture. The data syncs with her health profile in real-time, helping her meet her fitness goals. On her way, Ava passes through the city's green corridors, areas dedicated to outdoor activities. She sees groups of people engaged in yoga, running, and other forms of exercise. The city, once dominated by cars, is now a haven for cyclists and fitness enthusiasts. Ava's commute is not just a means of getting to work; it's a daily fitness routine, a way to connect with the city and a testament to how technology has seamlessly integrated cycling into everyday life. The city of 2035 is a city built for people like Ava, where technology, fitness, and sustainability intersect to create a healthier and more connected community.
