1 – Systems

Nowhere to Call Home

A system mapping of the housing search for people working in the informal economy in Lagos, Nigeria.

Journal Entry

An important question in my academic career has been “How can the Lagos State Government (LASG) effectively raise the living standards of its constituents and sustain economic growth without permanently displacing Lagos’ poorest residents?” After our first colloquium session, I have started to examine the current housing options for low-income denizens of Lagos as a collection of systems whose purposes include finding housing, providing housing, and regulating the housing market. The system I want to focus on right now is the one that identifies affordable housing in the Lagos metropolis for people who work in the informal economy or are a internally displaced due to conflict in other states. I’m struggling a little bit to define the boundaries of this system, because it is not well documented and it feels so closely connected to the systems that involve relocating to Lagos for economic opportunities or safety, looking for housing as a worker in the formal economy, and luxury development. There is little documented about this system but it seems to parallel the difficult searches undertaken by the working and middle class. Here is how some of the elements within this system interact. An individual, such as Stephanie Odili in a piece by Aljazeera, will meet with real estate agents to view the limited housing stock within their price range. At times the real estate agent will require a fee to show a space, which is illegal. The government does not enforce many of its housing-related laws. If a suitable place is found, a year’s worth of rent is needed to secure the lease. In a similar, but more enigmatic, way to New York City, a housing unit can rent out to someone else while you are still trying to secure it. The realtor pulling the rug from under you functions like a reinforcing feedback loop. One is sent back to square one or another option, finding a place to squat until one is evicted. This system is not resilient to economic and socio-political shocks. If and when luxury real estate developers decide that an informal settlement’s location is desirable, the government can evict the residents on the grounds of urban renewal. Housing security is also low when the already volatile economy is doing much worse, like it was during the Covid-19 pandemic. Landlords may choose to sell their land and properties and give tenants and squatters as little as one-day’s notice. If a resident loses their source of income due to government-mandated lockdowns, they can lose the right to occupy their housing unit once their lease-term is up.

2 – Authorship

Authorship in Lagos' Informal Settlements

Given the amorphous nature of the process of finding housing as a member of the informal economy or an internally displaced person, it is difficult to identify exactly where the lock-ins lie. It is possible that the absence of public housing, the resulting reliance on the private housing market, and the custom of paying annual rent upfront can all act as lock-ins. Housing options are limited, rent payment options are limited, and more flexibility should be granted to those seeking housing. The circumstances of the lives of the future residents of informal settlements preclude them from access to the forms of technology that provide some form of accountability and access to information. Landlords and realtors have too much flexibility within the system. They are at liberty to exploit prospective tenants because tenant laws are not enforced by a state government that relies on corruption to fatten the bellies of the leaders at nearly every level of seniority.

The lack of data provides the opportunity for the members of the informal economy and internally displaced Nigerians to become authors of a better, more equitable system that prioritizes their needs and upholds their inherent dignity as human beings. Other stakeholders have proven to prioritize their financial gain over the well-being of these two communities.

My authorship philosophy is still in its infancy, but I do draw inspiration from Karp Strategies’ Virtual Toolkit for Public Engagement. They focus on leveraging technologies to center the needs, ideas, and desires of the community.

I must (sheepishly) admit that I spent the past few days discussing AI and authorship outside of my system. My apologies, everyone! But do feel free to ask me about those thoughts too.
