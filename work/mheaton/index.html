1 – Systems

Modular Synthesis

A system mapping of creative voltages.

What is the system?

An obvious — friends would call this a little on the nose — application of systems theory I interact with on a regular basis is the synthesizer system that lives in my apartment. This system is made up of prefabricated units called “modules” that can be assembled according to a musician’s particular interests. The function of their system will change depending on 1) the initialization of modules into the system and 2) creative “patching” of signal across modules using removable 3.5mm cables. Depending on a user’s disposition, the system could be used to create dance music, pop, soundscapes, foley effects for film … many possibilities here.

The system passes audio-rate signals from module to module. These are sounds that, if connected to a speaker, would be perceptible to a listener. In addition, the system includes control voltages: literally electricity within designated minimum and maximum parameters (typically -5v to +8v). Control voltages (CV) are used to creatively alter the audio: its amount, its quality, its duration, etc. The “stock” of audio signals and control voltages alike can be purposely attenuated or amplified through resistor or gain circuits, but otherwise voltage drop-off between modules is negligible.

A typical synthesizer patch designates a flow from sound source, perhaps an oscillating wave whose frequency designates its pitch and whose amplitude gives it a volume. This sound can be leveraged in myriad ways - filtering harmonics away to the fundamental tone, changing its behavior over time, or mixing multiple channels at different amplitudes to simulate a space.

An interesting compositional tactic involves so-called generative musical techniques. By carefully arranging the flow of control voltages to ebb and flow durationally, commonly with an element of chance or randomness, the parameters of the system will unfurl into different configurations over time. For instance, boolean logic can be introduced to change a rhythm or sequence of pitches once certain parameters are satisfied. Feedback loops give the effect of an instrument playing itself, and can expand the palette and creative potential of a musician who may otherwise be performing alone.
